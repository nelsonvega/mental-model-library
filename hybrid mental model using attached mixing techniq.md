# generate 20 mental models to combine  fields, or ideas to genrate new ideas or models  using evolutionary algorithmic, mixing technique, creative technique using lateral thinking , multiple thinking hats or things like that

Here are 20 concise mental models you can use to systematically combine fields and generate new ideas, framed with evolutionary, mixing, and lateral-thinking techniques.[^1][^2][^3][^4]

***

## Evolutionary-style models

1. Mutation Stack
Start with a base idea and enforce small, random “mutations” on key parameters (who, what, where, business model, tech stack), then keep only variants that are strictly more interesting or feasible than the parent.[^5][^6]
2. Crossover Canvas
Take two existing solutions from different domains, map their components into a simple canvas (inputs, process, outputs, feedback), then cross-swap components to form hybrid architectures you wouldn’t design from scratch.[^7][^6]
3. Fitness Frontier
Define a multi-objective “fitness” (e.g., usefulness, delight, defensibility, feasibility) and evolve idea variants along a Pareto frontier, explicitly exploring tradeoffs instead of chasing a single “best” idea.[^5]
4. Weakness Breeder
Collect “weak cases” where existing models fail, then evolve ideas specifically optimized for those failure regions, using them as high-signal fitness tests for innovation.[^8][^9]
5. Ecosystem Niching
Treat your solution space as an ecosystem where each idea occupies a niche; deliberately search for under-populated niches (segments, workflows, constraints) and evolve specialized variants for each.[^6]

***

## Mixing and recombination models

6. SCAMPER Recombiner
Apply SCAMPER (Substitute, Combine, Adapt, Modify, Put to other use, Eliminate, Reverse) to two fields at once, forcing each SCAMPER action to draw one ingredient from each domain.[^10]
7. Forced Association Grid
Create a 2D grid: rows = concepts from field A, columns = concepts from field B; force yourself to propose at least one idea per cell, no matter how absurd at first pass.[^11][^10]
8. Pattern Template Transfer
Extract the abstract pattern from one domain (e.g., subscription, marketplace, just-in-time, compiler pipeline) and systematically instantiate it in another domain’s entities and flows.[^4]
9. Constraint Swap Model
Take the hard constraints from domain A (regulation, latency, safety) and impose them on domain B; design solutions that would work if B had A’s constraints, then relax back to reality.[^4]
10. Interface Inversion
List interfaces from one field (APIs, contracts, rituals) and invert the control direction in another field, designing ideas where the “passive” side becomes the orchestrator.[^4]

***

## Lateral and De Bono–style models

11. Six Hats Pipeline
Run a candidate idea explicitly through six passes: White (facts), Red (gut/emotion), Yellow (upside), Black (risks), Green (alternatives/variants), Blue (orchestration/roadmap), treating each pass as a separate transformation stage, not just evaluation.[^2][^12][^4]
12. Random Input Catalyst
Inject a random word/image/technology into your problem and require at least three plausible mappings from that random element into your system architecture, domain language, or business logic.[^1][^11]
13. Provocation Reversal
State a core assumption of your domain (“users should minimize risk”), reverse it (“users must take frequent small risks”), and design systems that would make that provocative world coherent and valuable.[^3][^1]
14. Parallel Worlds Analogy
Pick a “far” domain (ecology, music, sports) and map its roles, flows, and incentives 1:1 onto your target domain; ideas arise from where the analogy breaks and you patch it with new mechanisms.[^10][^4]
15. Constraint Removal Shock
Temporarily delete a central constraint (no regulation, infinite compute, zero marginal cost), generate options in that unconstrained world, then re-introduce constraints and adapt the few most powerful ideas back into reality.[^3][^4]

***

## Multi-perspective / meta models

16. Lens Rotation Stack
Define 4–6 lenses (economic, technical, emotional, narrative, ecosystem, ethical) and force one complete idea rewrite under each lens, keeping divergences as separate branches rather than collapsing back prematurely.[^13][^4]
17. Time-Hopping Model
Design the idea as if it must work in three time slices: 5 years past, present, and 5 years future; differences in assumptions and infrastructure reveal novel combinations and feature sets.[^13][^6]
18. Failure-First Inversion
Begin with a “post-mortem from the future” that lists how the idea failed on launch, then design a new variant that assumes those failures as givens and turns them into system features or new products.[^12][^13]
19. Stakeholder Gene Pool
Treat each stakeholder type as a “gene” contributing requirements, fears, and desires; recombine subsets of stakeholders into micro-products or features optimized for that specific gene mix.[^12]
20. Curriculum of Complexity
Order idea variants from trivial to wildly ambitious and design a curriculum where each stage funds, derisks, or trains data/models for the next, so the portfolio itself behaves like a learning evolutionary system.[^8][^13]
